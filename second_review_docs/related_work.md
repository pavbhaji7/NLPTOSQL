# Related Work

The quest to translate human language into database queries is not a new one. Researchers have spent decades trying to bridge the gap between plain English and structured SQL. Over the years, the approaches have evolved dramatically, transitioning from early heuristic methods to the deep learning era, and now moving toward more nuanced hybrid systems. Understanding this progression is crucial for seeing exactly why current Large Language Models still struggle with database tasks, and why structural guardrails are making a major comeback in modern systems.

In the earlier days of NL2SQL research, the dominant strategy was to use syntactic parsing and rigid rule-based synthesis. A notable example of this era is the work done on Sqlizer, where researchers attempted to synthesize queries by mapping natural language phrases directly to SQL fragments using predefined semantic rules [5]. These early systems had a major advantage: absolute precision. Because they were essentially hard-coded to expect certain sentence structures, if a query worked, it was guaranteed to be accurate. However, as noted in various literature reviews [3], [6], their fatal flaw was their rigidity. Natural language is messy, highly contextual, and unpredictable. If a user asked a question using a synonym the system hadn't been explicitly programmed to recognize, the translation would fail completely. This brittleness made them difficult to scale across different enterprise domains without constant, manual developer intervention.

As computing power grew and neural networks became the academic standard, the entire field underwent a massive paradigm shift. As Kumar et al. outlined in their extensive survey on the topic, the focus moved aggressively toward deep learning [4]. Researchers began treating text-to-SQL essentially as a foreign language translation problem—much like translating English to French—using sequence-to-sequence neural networks. Wong et al. also documented this transition, highlighting how machine learning implementations allowed systems to handle a much wider variety of sentence structures without needing hard-coded rules for every single conversational edge case [9]. 

This deep learning wave eventually culminated in the current era of Large Language Models (LLMs). However, while LLMs brought incredible conversational flexibility, researchers quickly realized that raw generative AI was a poor fit for the rigid, unforgiving syntax of SQL. Zhang et al. explored this heavily, noting that standard LLMs often fail to grasp the underlying architecture of a database, leading to structurally invalid queries [2]. Gan et al. echoed this sentiment in their work on Natural SQL, pointing out that inferring SQL directly from natural language specifications frequently leads to "hallucinations"—where the AI confidently invents columns or misinterprets foreign-key relationships [8]. 

Because generative models struggle so much with structural accuracy, there has been a significant resurgence in research focused on adding rules back into the mix. We are now seeing a push toward hybrid models that use NLP for understanding intent, but rely on deterministic rules to actually write the code. Tony et al. highlighted the renewed necessity of rule-based models to ensure logical consistency [7]. Building heavily on this concept, Shalaan et al. introduced the G-SQL framework, which proved that using a schema-aware, rule-guided approach could drastically improve robustness [1]. By forcing the NLP pipeline to cross-reference an actual database schema before generating SQL, they successfully eliminated the hallucination problem that plagues modern LLMs. Our own architecture draws heavy inspiration from this methodology, prioritizing strict schema-linking over pure probabilistic generation.

Finally, while most research has historically focused heavily on the backend algorithms, the user experience has often been treated as an afterthought. However, as Wu et al. demonstrated with their NL2viz system, translating natural language isn't just about generating code; it is about proving to the user that the code is correct [10]. Black-box systems fail to build user trust because they offer no explanation for their output. This insight directly influenced our decision to incorporate a pipeline visualizer in our frontend, ensuring that users can visually verify how their words were mapped to the database schema. By combining the schema-aware logic pioneered by recent rule-based research with a transparent user interface, our system addresses both the technical and human-centric challenges of NL2SQL translation.
