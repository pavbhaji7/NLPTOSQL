# Introduction

We live in an era defined by data. Look under the hood of almost any modern enterprise—whether it’s a hospital managing patient records, a retail giant tracking inventory, or a bank handling millions of daily transactions—and you will find a relational database. These databases are incredibly efficient at storing and organizing massive amounts of information. However, there is a massive catch: to actually get answers out of them, you have to speak their language. For decades, that language has been Structured Query Language (SQL). While SQL is undeniably powerful, it creates a steep learning curve that locks out the vast majority of business professionals, managers, and domain experts from directly interacting with their own data.

When non-technical staff need data to make decisions, they usually can't just pull it themselves. Instead, they have to submit a request to a data engineering or analytics team and wait. This back-and-forth process creates frustrating workflow bottlenecks. Analysts end up spending a massive chunk of their week writing basic, repetitive SQL queries instead of doing actual deep-dive analysis [3], [6]. To fix this broken workflow, the software industry and academic researchers have invested heavily in Natural Language Interfaces to Databases (NLIDB). The ultimate dream is straightforward: let a user type a question in everyday English, and have the computer automatically fetch the right numbers [9].

Looking back at how we tried to solve this in the past, early solutions were heavily reliant on strict rule-based synthesis. Systems like Sqlizer tried to map words to database columns using rigid heuristics [5]. While they were somewhat reliable in controlled environments, they were incredibly brittle in the real world. If a user phrased a question just slightly differently than what the developers hard-coded, the entire system would fail to generate a query.

Because of this lack of flexibility, the research community shifted gears dramatically. Over the last few years, the focus has moved almost entirely toward deep learning and, more recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) [4]. There is absolutely no denying that these modern models have made breathtaking leaps in conversational understanding. You can talk to them naturally, and they usually understand your intent. 

However, jumping straight to LLMs introduced a massive, unexpected problem: unpredictability. Modern AI models act essentially like "black boxes." They don't actually understand the mathematical structure of a database; they are just probabilistically predicting the next word in a sequence. Because of this, they are notoriously prone to hallucinations. They might confidently invent a table name that doesn't exist, misinterpret how two tables are connected, or spit out SQL code that technically looks right but fails to execute on the backend [2], [8]. In a casual setting, a hallucination is annoying. In an enterprise environment, where running a bad query could return entirely wrong financial numbers or crash a production database, it is a risk that organizations simply will not take.

This stark realization has sparked a major pivot in the field. Rather than throwing out rules entirely or relying completely on black-box AI, researchers are now looking at hybrid approaches. By combining the natural language understanding of modern NLP with strict, rule-guided schema linking, we can build systems that actually understand the blueprint of the database they are querying [1], [7].

In this project, we introduce a highly transparent, schema-aware pipeline designed specifically to convert natural language into SQL without the risks of AI hallucination. Instead of blindly trusting a black-box model to generate code, our approach breaks the process down into logical steps. We systematically parse the user's input, apply semantic tagging to identify key terms, and then use graph-based inference to automatically trace foreign-key relationships. This structural grounding acts as a guardrail, ensuring that every generated query strictly adheres to the actual database schema. 

Finally, we tackled what is arguably the biggest hurdle in deploying AI tools: user trust. If a user doesn't understand how a system got its answer, they won't trust it. To address this, we developed an interactive visualization frontend. This interface literally breaks down the "thought process" of our NLP engine, showing the user exactly how their everyday sentence was mapped to specific tables and columns before any SQL is executed [10]. Ultimately, this project delivers a reliable, transparent, and hallucination-free way for non-experts to interact with complex databases.
